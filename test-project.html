Sortris

A casual, grid-style, physics-based interior design game.

Engine: Unreal Engine 5.5.4 Platform: PC (Windows) Role: Programmer, 3D Artist Team: 5 Members Duration: Game Jam (2025 Games Now! #17) Theme: CUTE
Download Game

Project Overview

SORTRIS is a game where you take the role of an interior designer but with a twist - the furniture falls into place! Help out five clients in designing their dream rooms and organize their stuff however you want. Get creative and try your best to give these clients the room they deserve in this cute but hectic experience!

Sortris was created by a team of five for the Games Now!'s #17 Game Jam 2025. The theme for this game jam is CUTE. This game is a collaboration of game developers between different nationalities: Pakistan, Indonesia, Nigeria, and the Philippines.

The core gameplay revolves around a Tetris-inspired grid system where furniture pieces drop from above and the player must arrange them within a room to satisfy each client's needs. The physics-based interactions add an extra layer of chaos and charm to the interior design process.

For full disclosure, this game does not contain any content derived or produced by generative AI tools. It does not feature mature content and/or themes, and all copyrighted material is credited in the documentation.

Grid-Style Movement: Tetris-Inspired Room Arrangement

The core mechanic of Sortris is a grid-style movement system inspired by classic Tetris gameplay. Furniture pieces fall from above into a designated room space, and the player must strategically position and rotate each piece to create a functional and aesthetically pleasing layout.

Each piece interacts with the grid and other objects through physics-based collisions, meaning placement is not just about alignment but also about managing how items stack, tumble, and settle. This creates a dynamic and sometimes chaotic design process where careful planning meets emergent physical behavior.

The grid system ensures that players have clear spatial constraints to work within, while the physics engine adds unpredictability and charm. A piece that looks perfectly placed might wobble and shift when another item lands nearby, forcing the player to adapt their strategy on the fly.

This combination of structured grid movement and organic physics results in a gameplay loop that is easy to understand but offers depth through the interplay of planning and reactive problem-solving.

Photo Mode: Capture Your Design at the End of Each Round

At the end of each design round, players can activate the in-game photo mode to capture a screenshot of their final room arrangement. This snapshot serves as a memento of the player's creative work and can be saved or shared.

The photo mode was designed to give players a sense of accomplishment and a way to document their unique interior designs. Each client's room will look different depending on the player's choices, making every screenshot a one-of-a-kind creation.

The snapshot system captures the full room state, including all placed furniture, their positions, rotations, and any physics-driven settling that occurred during the round. This ensures that the final image accurately represents the player's completed design.

Future iterations could expand this system to include filters, camera angles, and social sharing options, turning each completed room into a shareable piece of player-generated content.

Target Comparison System: Match the Reference, Score Your Design (Planned)

A planned future feature for Sortris introduces a target-based scoring system that adds a competitive and evaluative layer to the interior design gameplay. At the start of each round, the player will be shown a reference photograph displaying the ideal or target arrangement of furniture for that client's room.

This reference image will remain visible in the UI throughout the gameplay session, serving as a visual guide that the player can consult while placing and arranging items. The goal is to recreate the target layout as closely as possible within the constraints of the falling-piece mechanic and physics interactions.

When the round concludes, the player will use the existing photo mode to capture a screenshot of their final arrangement. The system will then perform an automated comparison between the player's screenshot and the original target image, analyzing spatial alignment, furniture placement, and overall room composition.

Based on this comparison, the game will generate a similarity score that rewards the player for how closely their design matches the reference. Higher accuracy yields better scores, encouraging replayability as players strive to perfect their arrangements. This system transforms the freeform creative experience into a skill-based challenge where precision and spatial reasoning are key.

This feature is currently in the conceptual stage and represents a natural evolution of the existing snapshot mechanics, bridging creative expression with objective evaluation.
